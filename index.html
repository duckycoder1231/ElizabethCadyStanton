Elizabeth Cady Stanton

Background Information

Elizabeth Cady Stanton (November 12, 1815 – October 26, 1902) was an American writer and activist who was a leader in the women's rights movement. She was the main force behind the Seneca Falls Convention, the first women's rights convention in the United States, and was the primary author of its Declaration of Sentiments. Stanton also played a key role in organizing the women's suffrage movement in the United States.

Accomplishments

Stanton was instrumental in the early women's rights movement. She co-organized the Seneca Falls Convention in 1848, where the Declaration of Sentiments was signed, demanding equal rights for women. She advocated for women's suffrage, property rights, and education. Stanton helped draft the 14th and 15th Amendments to the U.S. Constitution but was disappointed that they did not include women. She wrote extensively, including "The Woman's Bible," which reinterpreted the Bible from a feminist perspective. Her work laid the foundation for the 19th Amendment, granting women the right to vote.

Death and Legacy

Elizabeth Cady Stanton died on October 26, 1902, in New York City at the age of 86. She is buried in Woodlawn Cemetery in the Bronx. Her legacy endures as one of the most influential figures in American feminism. She inspired generations of women to fight for equality and is credited with helping to secure women's suffrage in the United States. Today, she is remembered as a trailblazer who challenged societal norms and advanced the cause of gender equality.

Fun Facts

  • Stanton was the first woman to run for the U.S. Congress in 1866, though she was not allowed to vote for herself.
  • She had seven children and managed to balance motherhood with her activism.
  • Stanton traveled extensively in Europe and was influenced by international women's rights movements.
  • She was known for her sharp wit and often used humor in her speeches and writings.
